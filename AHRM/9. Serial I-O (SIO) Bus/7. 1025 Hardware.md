## 9.7 1025 Hardware

Under the hood, the 1025 is based on an 8051 microcontroller running at 7.37MHz, 4K of firmware in external ROM, and a 8155 port extender providing additional I/O and 256 bytes of RAM.

## Firmware revisions

There is one known firmware revision for the 1025, with a CRC32 of [46D194B4].

## SIO bus communication

The 8051's serial port is used for communication across the SIO bus, so the firmware doesn't have to bit-bang the serial bus data. It is driven in mode 0, giving a baud rate of 7.37MHz ÷ 12 ÷ 32 = 19.192KHz. The firmware does not use RI/TI interrupts and uses polling for both receive and transmit.

The SIO command signal is connected to the timer 0 (T0) input, which is driven low when SIO command is asserted. It too is polled by the firmware.

SIO ready is connected to the INT1 input and does use an interrupt. The firmware powers down the motors and idles the print mechanism while waiting for the computer to turn on.

## Horizontal positioning

The print head is horizontally positioned by the space motor under MCU control, sequentially activating motor phases to move the print head back and forth. However, the space motor is not a stepper motor, and print columns aren't aligned to the phases. Instead, the firmware uses software delays to time the print columns accurately between the phases, and also varies the speed.

Every four steps turns the motor shaft 30° and moves the print head by 2.54 mm or 1/10th of an inch. 55 The phases are numbered such that activating ascending phases (1, 2, …) moves the print head left and activating descending numbered phases move the print head right.

In 5 CPI and 10 CPI modes, the firmware activates pairs of adjacent phases at a time, stepping phases every ~4060 machine cycles. One machine cycle on the 8051 takes 12 T-states, so this is about 38 characters/second. Columns are printed 1350 cycles apart in 10 CPI mode for 12 columns per character. This timing is doubled to 2700 cycles per column in 5 CPI mode. The head is returned to home position slightly more slowly at 4120 cycles per phase step, also activating pairs of phases at a time.

In 16.5 CPI mode, the firmware alternates stepping with single and paired phases, i.e. 4, 4+3, 3, 3+2, 2, 2+1, 1, 1+4. The motor has more torque when two phases are activated, so the firmware compensates by varying the time delay between steps, using ~2040 cycles with two phases and ~4700 cycles with single phase. Columns are still printed 1350 cycles apart, so the character rate is the same but the head movement rate slows proportionally to the higher density.
